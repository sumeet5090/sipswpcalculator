<h2 id="what-are-capital-gains">What Are Capital Gains? (The Simple Explanation)</h2>

<p>Imagine you buy a toy for ₹100 and sell it later for ₹150. The ₹50 extra you made is called a <strong>"capital gain"</strong> — it's the profit you earned on something you owned. The government says, "Since you made money, you need to share a small part of it as tax."</p>

<p>Now, mutual funds work the same way. When you invest money in a mutual fund, the fund manager buys stocks, bonds, or other assets with your money. Over time, the value of your investment grows. When you sell (or "redeem") your mutual fund units, the difference between what you paid (the <strong>purchase price</strong>) and what you received (the <strong>sale price</strong>) is your capital gain — and that's what gets taxed.</p>

<p>There are two types of capital gains:</p>
<ul>
    <li><strong>Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG):</strong> Profit made when you sell an investment you held for a <em>short time</em>. For equity mutual funds, "short" means less than 12 months.</li>
    <li><strong>Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG):</strong> Profit made when you sell an investment you held for a <em>long time</em>. For equity mutual funds, "long" means 12 months or more.</li>
</ul>

<p>The logic is simple: the government <em>rewards</em> you for holding investments longer by charging a lower tax rate on long-term gains. This is why patient investors always pay less tax than frequent traders.</p>

<p>If you redeem 200 units in March 2026, FIFO means:</p>
<ul>
    <li><strong>First 100 units</strong> (from Jan 2025) → <strong>LTCG at 12.5%</strong> (held > 12 months) ✅</li>

<p>All 200 units qualify for the lower LTCG rate because your <em>oldest</em> SIPs are sold first. This is why SIPs naturally become more tax-efficient the longer you invest.</p>

<h2 id="ltcg-harvesting">Tax-Loss & LTCG Harvesting: The ₹1.25 Lakh Strategy</h2>

<p>Every financial year, the first ₹1.25 lakh of equity LTCG is <strong>completely tax-free</strong>. Smart investors use this exemption proactively through a technique called <strong>"LTCG Harvesting"</strong>.</p>

<h3 id="how-harvesting-works">How It Works (Step-by-Step)</h3>

<ol>
    <li><strong>Near year-end (January–March),</strong> calculate your unrealized long-term gains across all equity mutual funds.</li>
    <li><strong>Redeem units</strong> worth up to ₹1.25 lakh in gains. Since this is within the exemption, you pay <strong>zero tax</strong>.</li>
    <li><strong>Immediately reinvest</strong> the full redemption amount into the same or a similar fund.</li>
    <li><strong>Result:</strong> Your cost basis has been "reset" upward. Future gains are calculated from the higher reinvestment price, reducing your future LTCG liability.</li>
</ol>

<h3 id="harvesting-example">Worked Example: 10-Year Harvesting Impact</h3>

<p>Suppose you invest ₹5 lakh in an equity fund at NAV ₹100 (5,000 units). After 2 years, NAV is ₹140.</p>

<ul>
    <li><strong>Without harvesting:</strong> Your gains are ₹2 lakh. You'll pay 12.5% on ₹75,000 (₹2L − ₹1.25L exemption) = <strong>₹9,375 tax</strong>.</li>
    <li><strong>With annual harvesting:</strong> In Year 1, you redeem ₹1.25L of gains (tax-free) and reinvest. Your new cost basis is higher. In Year 2, you repeat. By the time you actually need the money, most of your gains have already been "harvested" — your total tax bill is <strong>₹0</strong>.</li>
</ul>

<p>Over a 10-year investment, systematic annual harvesting can save you <strong>₹1.5 lakh to ₹3 lakh</strong> in taxes on a ₹25-lakh portfolio. It's free money — all you need is the discipline to do it every March.</p>

<h2 id="new-vs-old-regime">New vs Old Tax Regime: Impact on Mutual Fund Investors</h2>

<p>India introduced a simplified "New Tax Regime" with lower slab rates but no deductions. Here's how it affects mutual fund investors:</p>

<ul>
    <li><strong>Capital gains tax rates are the SAME</strong> under both regimes. Whether you choose Old or New Regime, your mutual fund LTCG is still 12.5% and STCG is still 20%.</li>
    <li><strong>The difference matters for ELSS:</strong> Under the Old Regime, you can claim a Section 80C deduction (up to ₹1.5 lakh) for ELSS investments. Under the New Regime, this deduction is <em>not available</em>. If you're on the New Regime, ELSS has no tax-saving advantage over any other equity fund.</li>
    <li><strong>Dividend taxation:</strong> Regardless of regime, all mutual fund dividends are added to your income and taxed at your slab rate.</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="common-mistakes">5 Common Mutual Fund Tax Mistakes to Avoid</h2>

<ol>
    <li><strong>Redeeming before 12 months:</strong> This is the most expensive mistake. Even if you need cash urgently, selling equity funds at 11 months instead of 13 months means paying 20% STCG instead of 12.5% LTCG (or even 0% within the exemption).</li>
    <li><strong>Ignoring TDS on debt funds:</strong> For NRIs, mutual fund companies deduct TDS (Tax Deducted at Source) of 20% on STCG and 12.5% on LTCG for equity funds. Failure to account for this can cause cash flow surprises.</li>
    <li><strong>Not harvesting the ₹1.25 lakh exemption:</strong> Most investors leave this exemption unused every year. It's literally free tax savings that requires 15 minutes of work in March.</li>
    <li><strong>Choosing dividend option over growth:</strong> Dividends are added to your income and taxed at your slab rate (up to 30%). Growth option + SWP is almost always more tax-efficient because only the gain portion of SWP withdrawals is taxed.</li>
    <li><strong>Treating all mutual funds the same:</strong> Debt funds, international funds, and gold funds have completely different tax rules than equity funds. A "mutual fund" is not a single tax category.</li>
</ol>

<h2 id="faq">Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

<h3>How much LTCG is tax-free on mutual funds in 2026?</h3>
<p>For equity mutual funds in India, the first <strong>₹1.25 lakh</strong> of long-term capital gains (gains on units held for over 12 months) is exempt from tax every financial year. This exemption applies per PAN (individual level), not per fund. Gains above ₹1.25 lakh are taxed at 12.5%.</p>

<h3>Is STCG taxed at 15% or 20% in 2026?</h3>
<p>As of the Budget 2024 amendments (effective for FY 2026-27), <strong>STCG on equity mutual funds is taxed at 20%</strong>. The earlier rate of 15% was increased. STCG applies when you sell equity fund units held for less than 12 months.</p>

<h3>Are SWP withdrawals fully taxable?</h3>
<p>No. Each SWP withdrawal consists of two parts: (1) your <strong>original principal</strong> (not taxable) and (2) the <strong>capital gain</strong> (taxable). In the early years of an SWP, the gain component is very small because most of what you withdraw is your own invested money. This makes SWP significantly more tax-efficient than FD interest, which is 100% taxable.</p>

<h3>How are debt mutual funds taxed after 2023?</h3>
<p>For debt mutual funds purchased after April 1, 2023, there is <strong>no distinction between STCG and LTCG</strong>. All gains are taxed at your income slab rate (up to 30%), regardless of how long you held the fund. The indexation benefit that previously applied to debt funds has been completely removed.</p>

<h3>Do I need to pay tax if I switch between mutual funds?</h3>
<p>Yes. A switch (e.g., from one equity fund to another) is treated as a <strong>redemption + new purchase</strong>. The redemption triggers capital gains tax on any profit in the original fund. This is true even if the switch is within the same AMC (fund house).</p>

<h3>How are ELSS funds taxed under the New Tax Regime?</h3>
<p>Under the New Tax Regime, you <strong>cannot claim the Section 80C deduction</strong> for ELSS investments. However, the capital gains tax treatment remains the same: LTCG at 12.5% (after 12 months) with ₹1.25 lakh exemption, and STCG at 20%. If you're on the New Regime, ELSS offers no tax-saving benefit during the investment phase.</p>

<h3>Is there a surcharge on mutual fund capital gains?</h3>
<p>Yes, for high-income individuals. If your total income (including capital gains) exceeds ₹50 lakh, a surcharge of 10-37% is applied on top of the base tax rate. Additionally, a 4% Health & Education Cess applies on the total tax + surcharge amount. For most retail investors below ₹50 lakh income, the effective rates are exactly as stated in the tables above.</p>

<h3>How do I report mutual fund gains in my ITR?</h3>
<p>Mutual fund capital gains must be reported in your Income Tax Return. Use <strong>ITR-2</strong> (or ITR-3 if you have business income). List each redemption under <strong>Schedule CG (Capital Gains)</strong>. Your mutual fund AMC will provide a <strong>Capital Gains Statement</strong> (usually downloadable from their website or via CAMS/KFintech portals) that shows the exact purchase dates, sale dates, and computed gains for each transaction.</p>
